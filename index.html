High Museum Skylights

Ian Droessler, Hannah Lu, Sage McGill, Tyler Nguyen

Foundations and Background

In the Renzo Piano buildings of the High Museum of Art in Atlanta, Georgia, there are one thousand skylights overlooking many of the museum's artworks. The buildings were designed by the Renzo Piano Building Workshop (in collaboration with Lord, Aeck & Sargent Inc.) from 1999 to 2003 (High Museum of Art Expansion, n.d.). The Wieland Pavilion and Anne Cox Chambers Wing features these skylights (High Museum of Art, n.d.), but in the last few years, these skylights have begun leaking.

Each skylight channels light from the north and into the museum below, and each skylight is partially covered by a large hood (Great Museums Television, n.d.). We have been unable to acquire high-quality and close-up images of the skylights and images of the skylights' design, but we believe there to be rubber gasket used to seal the gap between the skylight's glass and the outer structure based on images we are able to access. (We have also seen people constructing the skylights towards the end of construction, and there appears to be some kind of seal along the glass' rim.) Because of these things, we believe that the gasket has worn and become dysfunctional, supported by a guide from Bergstrom Industries that presents this explanation for skylight leakage (Naas, n.d.).

We generally lack an understanding of the requirements from the High regarding possible solutions to this issue and lack assured communication with them, but we have attempted to design a solution that considers time, resources, and finances.

The Product

Because we lack high-quality images of the skylights' design, we could only make estimations of the dimensions of the skylight, and our product's design reflects this accordingly.

Our solution is to create a replacement gasket for the skylights' glass. This is because we do not have information about the limitations to our solution, but we believe at least sourcing replacement gaskets and having them installed to be a financially efficient and resource efficient solution that could be implemented.
